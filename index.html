When is the next leap second?

No leap second is currently scheduled. The last one was inserted at the end of 31 December 2016, and none has been needed since. The IERS (International Earth Rotation and Reference Systems Service) decides twice a year, publishing the verdict in Bulletin C, whether to add one at the end of the coming June or December. Skew counts down to the 31 December 2026 boundary, the next decision point, and tracks the running odds of each outcome with the reasoning behind them.

Three clocks can all be right at once because they treat leap seconds differently. TAI (International Atomic Time) never adds them, so the UTC to TAI offset has sat at a fixed 37 seconds since 2016. GPS time also skips leap seconds; it was aligned with UTC in 1980 and now runs 18 seconds ahead of it, exactly 19 seconds behind TAI. Skew draws all three ticking side by side so the gap is something you watch rather than take on faith from a table.

Leap second FAQ

When is the next leap second?
None is scheduled yet. The next opportunity is the end of December 2026, and IERS will confirm or rule it out in Bulletin C. Every bulletin since 2016 has called for no leap second, because Earth's rotation has been trending faster rather than slower.
What is the UTC to TAI offset right now?
37 seconds. TAI has been 37 seconds ahead of UTC since the last leap second at the end of 2016, and that gap only moves when a new leap second is added or skipped. GPS time sits 18 seconds ahead of UTC and 19 seconds behind TAI.
What is the Skew leap second countdown counting down to?
The last second of 31 December 2026 UTC, the next boundary at which a leap second could be inserted or, for the first time ever, skipped. When the boundary passes, the page switches to a decided state pointing you to Bulletin C for the confirmed outcome.
Could the next leap second be negative?
It is possible. Earth's rotation has sped up in recent years, so rather than adding a second the world may eventually remove one, a negative leap second that has never happened before. Skew's odds tracker gives that outcome a small but real probability for 2026, alongside the 2022 CGPM vote to retire leap seconds entirely by 2035.
